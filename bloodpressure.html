Blood Pressure

History of Blood Pressure

Early attempts to measure blood pressure were made during this period. Italian physician Santorio Santorio is credited with inventing the first blood pressure measuring device, a water-filled tube attached to a hollow reed, which he called a "pulsilogium."

Causes

The primary causes of blood pressure include:

  • Unhealthy diet high in sodium and saturated fats.
  • Lack of physical activity.
  • Excessive alcohol consumption.
  • Smoking and exposure to secondhand smoke.
  • Chronic stress.
  • Genetic predisposition.
  • Aging.
  • Obesity.
  • Chronic conditions like kidney disease, diabetes, and sleep apnea.
  • Certain medications.
  • Hormonal imbalances.

Symptoms

Some common symptoms of high blood pressure include:

  • Headaches, especially at the back of the head.
  • Blurred or double vision.
  • Chest pain or discomfort.
  • Shortness of breath.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness.
  • Nausea or vomiting.
  • Nosebleeds (less common).

Treatment and Cures

Treatment of high blood pressure may include:

  • Lifestyle modifications such as a healthy diet low in sodium and saturated fats, regular exercise, maintaining a healthy weight, limiting alcohol intake, and quitting smoking.
  • Medications like diuretics, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, beta-blockers, or calcium channel blockers.
  • Regular monitoring of blood pressure at home and attending check-ups with a healthcare provider.
  • Stress management techniques and complementary therapies under medical supervision.

Prevention Strategies

To prevent high blood pressure, consider the following strategies:

  • Maintain a healthy weight through diet and exercise.
  • Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
  • Stay physically active with regular exercise.
  • Monitor blood pressure levels regularly, especially if at risk.
  • Know your family history and other risk factors.
  • Manage stress and avoid tobacco.
  • Limit alcohol consumption.
  • Get regular check-ups for early detection.

Rate of Death

High blood pressure is estimated to account for about 13% of all deaths worldwide.
